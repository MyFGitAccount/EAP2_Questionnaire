### Recommendations

Based on the survey results and academic literature, suggestions can be made for students, service providers, and educational institutions.

**For students**  
Students need to actively manage their subscriptions because these systems are designed to make people forget. **Subscription fatigue** (Oteri et al., 2024) and the tendency to underestimate total spending—often more than $1,600 a year—show why oversight is important. Simple tools like calendar reminders, budgeting apps, or spreadsheets can help track renewal dates and free trial deadlines. Hochstein et al. (2021) explain that while recurring revenue models shift risk to sellers, students are still vulnerable because switching costs are low and managing multiple subscriptions is mentally demanding. Setting aside one day each month to review all subscriptions can help keep spending visible. Students should also look for student discounts before subscribing. Oteri et al. (2024) note that free trials are often designed to make people forget to cancel, using psychological tactics like the anchoring effect and perceived fairness.

**For service providers**  
Companies can build trust with students by making cancellation simple, such as a “click-to-cancel” option. Oteri et al. (2024) show that **pricing transparency** and fairness are key factors influencing adoption and retention. Making cancellation as easy as signing up would set a brand apart in a market where cancellation is often frustrating. Providers should also recognize that **satiation drives cancellation**—when users feel they’ve had enough, they leave. Sapru (2025) suggests that providers can reduce churn by sending updates about new content, offering personalized recommendations, and adding pause features. His guardrailed elasticity pricing framework shows that flexible pricing for sensitive groups, like students, reduces cancellations while maximizing long-term customer lifetime value (CLV).

**For educational institutions**  
Schools like HKU SPACE can help by teaching **financial literacy** so students can spot and resist manipulative business practices. Hochstein et al. (2021) point out that customer success management (CSM) exists to prepare customers for value co-creation, but students don’t have a similar advocate. Teaching concepts from behavioral economics—like the **pain of paying, loss aversion, and the sunk-cost fallacy**—would give students tools to make smarter financial choices. Oteri et al. (2024) argue that understanding customer behavior and pricing elasticity is essential for product managers; students with the same mindset can become more careful consumers. Colleges could also negotiate licenses for software like Adobe Creative Cloud and Microsoft 365 and provide them free or at low cost. This would reduce the need for individual subscriptions and ease financial pressure, aligning with the value co-creation principles described by Hochstein et al. (2021).
