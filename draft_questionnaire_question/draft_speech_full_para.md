# SLIDE 3: Background – Hook

**Topic:** The Subscription Economy – Opening Hook

> 🟢 **Brian:** My name is Brian Cheung, and let me start by asking all of you a question. How many subscriptions are you currently paying for right now? Take a moment to think about it. Do you have Netflix? Disney Plus? Spotify? Apple Music? ChatGPT? Cloud storage like iCloud or Google Drive? Gaming subscriptions like Xbox Game Pass? Software subscriptions like Adobe Creative Cloud? Now, here is the real question that I want you to ask yourself. Do you actually know exactly how much money you are spending each month on all of these subscriptions combined? For many of us, the answer is probably no. According to Cobzaru and Tugui, who published a comprehensive study on the subscription economy in 2024, the average consumer now spends approximately one hundred thirty-three US dollars per month on subscription services. To put that number into perspective, that adds up to over sixteen hundred US dollars every single year, and that is just on subscriptions.

---

# SLIDE 4: Background – The Big Picture

**Topic:** The Subscription Economy – Market Growth and Student Relevance

> 🟢 **Brian:** And let me be clear that this is not just a small or temporary trend. According to the same researchers, Cobzaru and Tugui from 2024, the subscription economy is growing at an annual rate of eighteen percent. By the year 2025, they predict that the subscription market will reach a value of one point five trillion US dollars. Think about that for a moment. Everything around us is becoming a subscription – from the software we use for school to the music we listen to and the movies we watch. So why does this matter specifically for students? According to Kennedy and colleagues from their 2008 study on technology use among university students, nearly all university students own smartphones, which is ninety-six percent, and computers, which is eighty-nine percent. This means that students are early adopters of technology. However, most of us have very limited budgets. The question that guided our research is this: How do students with limited financial resources navigate this subscription-heavy world? That is exactly what we wanted to find out.

---

# SLIDE 5: Research Objectives

**Topic:** Research Objectives

> 🟠 **Rex:** My name is Rex Lui, and let me explain our research objectives. Our research had four main goals. First, we wanted to identify what types of online subscription services HKU SPACE students actually use in their daily lives. Second, we wanted to understand what factors influence their decisions to subscribe to a new service and what causes them to cancel. Third, we wanted to evaluate students' awareness and use of cost-saving strategies, such as student discounts or account sharing. Fourth, we wanted to assess their overall attitude toward the subscription model, especially when compared to traditional one-time purchases. So the central question we are asking is this: Do students love subscriptions, tolerate them because they are convenient, or secretly hate the subscription trend altogether?

---

# SLIDE 6: Method

**Topic:** Research Method

> 🟠 **Rex:** Now let me explain how we collected our data. We used a Microsoft Forms questionnaire as our research tool. Our target population was full-time students enrolled at HKU SPACE Community College. We distributed the survey between March ninth and March twentieth, 2026. In total, we received thirty-seven completed responses. Our questionnaire contained twenty-four questions organized into six sections. These sections covered demographics, subscription service usage, factors influencing subscription decisions, cancellation behaviors, payment preferences, and cost-saving strategies. We used a mix of multiple-choice questions, Likert scale questions, and select-all-that-apply questions to ensure comprehensive data collection.

According to Yoganarasimhan and colleagues from their 2023 study on free trials and subscription design, this finding reflects a broader trend across Asia. # SLIDE 11: The Ownership Paradox – Introduction

**Topic:** Introduction to the Ownership Paradox

> 🟣 **Anson:** My name is Anson Chan, and now I want to introduce you to something that researchers call the "ownership paradox." This is where our findings become really interesting. Students use subscriptions frequently, but do they actually like the subscription model itself? According to Yoganarasimhan and colleagues from their 2023 study, there is a fascinating contradiction in how consumers behave with digital products. Let me quote their exact finding. They write that consumers subscribe to digital products not necessarily because they prefer subscriptions, but because subscriptions are convenient or necessary. In other words, students may be using subscriptions, but that does not mean they are happy about the subscription trend. Let me show you what our data revealed about this paradox.

# SLIDE 13: Payment Preferences – Analysis

**Topic:** Payment Method Preferences Analysis

> 🟣 **Anson:** When we asked students about their payment method preference for software and tools, the results were very clear. Ten respondents, or thirty-one percent, said they strongly prefer one-time purchases. Thirteen respondents, or forty-one percent, said they somewhat prefer one-time purchases. This means that over seventy percent of our respondents prefer one-time purchases over subscription payments. In contrast, only four respondents, or twelve percent, said they somewhat prefer subscriptions. Five respondents, or sixteen percent, expressed no preference. Let me put this simply. Students use subscriptions because they are convenient or because they have no other choice. But when you give them a real choice, they would rather buy something once and own it forever than deal with recurring payments.

# SLIDE 14: The Critical Spending Group Analysis

**Topic:** Analysis of the HKD 2,001-4,000 Spending Group

> 🟣 **Anson:** Let me now focus on our largest spending group. Remember that twenty-seven students who spend between two thousand and one and four thousand Hong Kong dollars per month? This group represents seventy-three percent of our respondents. When we looked specifically at their payment preferences, we found that seventeen of these students preferred one-time purchases, while only four preferred subscriptions. According to Neunhoeffer from a 2023 study on subscription traps and consumer preferences, this finding makes perfect sense. Neunhoeffer argues that when consumers try to determine the value of a subscription service over time, they have great difficulty predicting its long-term future value. As a result, consumers will be more inclined to choose a one-time transaction because it provides them with certainty about the cost and financial clarity. When your monthly budget is tightly capped at two to four thousand dollars, paying once gives you certainty. Subscriptions feel risky.

# SLIDE 18: Low-Cost Subscriptions – Analysis

> 🟡 **Lihe:** We asked students how likely they would be to keep a subscription that costs under thirty Hong Kong dollars per year. They are also paying with mental energy and attention. This mental exhaustion is what Nguyen calls subscription fatigue.

This mental exhaustion that comes from trying to track everything manually is exactly why students develop such a cautious and conservative attitude toward taking on new subscription obligations.

This shows that students with moderate financial constraints are still highly motivated to access these platforms through legitimate channels, as long as the pricing meets their budget. The second most used strategy is account sharing. Eleven students reported sharing accounts with friends, and another eleven students reported sharing family plans. According to Lee and colleagues from their 2024 study on password-sharing behavior, students rationalize this type of sharing as a form of community solidarity. They see it as helping each other out rather than as something wrong. Finally, nine students reported setting scheduled reminders to cancel free trials, which is a proactive defense mechanism against unwanted automatic renewal charges.

# SLIDE 26: Overall Attitudes – Analysis

That means twenty-two respondents hold negative views toward the subscription trend. In contrast, only seven respondents said they were slightly positive, and only one respondent said they were very positive. Seven respondents were neutral. According to Baek and Kim from their 2022 study on consumer perceptions of subscription models, when an existing product changes to a subscription model, people's purchase intentions shift from yes toward no. This happens because consumers rely less on pre-purchase information to judge quality and more on actual use experience. Our study shows the same pattern. Students use subscription services frequently in their daily lives, but they hold a generally negative view of the trend where products and services are shifting to subscription models. Frequent use does not mean strong support.

# SLIDE 27: Recommendations – Systematic Level

**Topic:** Systematic Recommendations

> 🟣 **Anson:** Based on our findings, I want to offer some recommendations. First, at the systematic level, we recommend that policies should be adapted to modern commercial practices. Based on the European Commission's 2024 research on digital fairness, which studied consumer protection in the digital economy, we have three specific recommendations. First, governments should require transparent billing and pricing schemes so that consumers can see exactly what they are paying for with no hidden fees. Second, governments should ban deceptive website designs and manipulative marketing practices, which are often called dark patterns. These are tricks that companies use to make it hard for you to cancel or to trick you into paying more. Third, governments should require a simple and straightforward cancellation process, ideally one-click cancellation that is as easy as signing up was. The proposed European Union Digital Fairness Act addresses exactly these issues, and Hong Kong could benefit from similar regulations.

---

# SLIDE 28: Recommendations – Personal Level

**Topic:** Personal Recommendations for Students

> 🟡 **Lihe:** Second, at the personal level, students can also adopt several strategies to protect their own interests. Based on Nguyen's 2025 research on subscription fatigue, I recommend four things. First, install adblockers on your browsers to improve user interface clarity and reduce distracting advertisements. Second, utilize subscription tracking tools or budgeting applications. Remember what Tim showed you earlier – the one student who used a budgeting app had zero forgotten renewals. That is powerful evidence. Third, always check the final price before you click checkout to avoid hidden fees or sneaked-in items that companies add without your clear consent. Fourth, students should take the initiative to research and train themselves on financial and digital literacy. The more you understand how these systems work, the less likely you are to be taken advantage of. Better business practices combined with smarter personal habits will lead to less stressful subscription experiences.

# SLIDE 29: Limitations

**Topic:** Study Limitations

> 🔵 **Tim:** Before I conclude, I need to be honest about our study's limitations. First, with only thirty-seven respondents, our sample size is relatively small. This means that our findings may not represent the entire HKU SPACE student body. Second, our findings are based on self-reported data. This means that respondents might not accurately remember their behaviors, which is called recall bias. Additionally, respondents might have the tendency to provide answers that they believe appear more favorable rather than being fully honest, which is called social desirability bias. Third, because our study is limited to only one institution, the results may not apply to other universities in Hong Kong. Fourth, since attitudes were measured at only one point in time, our study cannot capture how perceptions or behaviors change over the course of a student's academic career or in response to shifting economic conditions. These limitations mean that we should be cautious about generalizing our findings too broadly.

---

# SLIDE 30: Conclusion

**Topic:** Summary of Key Findings

> 🔵 **Tim:** Now let me summarize what we learned from this research. First, HKU SPACE students are active subscription users, especially for entertainment services like video and music streaming. Second, students approach subscriptions with caution and pragmatism rather than enthusiasm. Third, cost is the main reason for cancellation, but even low-cost subscriptions get cancelled if students do not use them frequently. Fourth, students strongly prefer one-time purchases over recurring payments because they want to avoid the financial uncertainty and the mental burden of tracking multiple subscriptions. Fifth, students use smart cost-saving strategies, including student discounts, account sharing with friends and family, and setting reminders to cancel free trials. Sixth, and most importantly, the overall attitude toward the subscription trend is generally negative. We found twenty-two negative responses compared to only eight positive responses. Frequent use of subscription services does not necessarily reflect strong support for the subscription trend.

---

# SLIDE 31: Final Takeaway

**Topic:** Final Takeaway and Closing Remarks

> 🔵 **Tim:** My final takeaway is this. Students are strategic consumers. They use subscriptions when subscriptions are convenient or necessary. But given a real choice, they would rather buy something once and own it forever. For service providers, this means that success depends on respecting students' budget-conscious and strategic approach. Companies need to demonstrate value continuously and make cancellation easy, because that actually builds trust. For students, my advice is to stay vigilant, use tracking tools, set reminders for free trials, and remember that you do not have to love subscriptions just because you use them. Thank you for listening.
