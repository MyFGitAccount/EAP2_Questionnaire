# SLIDE 2: Agenda

> 🔵 **Tim:** Here's what we'll cover today. We'll start with some background on why this topic matters, then explain our research method, look at who responded to our survey, and dive into our key findings. We'll also share our recommendations and limitations before wrapping up with a conclusion.

# SLIDE 3: Background – Hook

> 🟢 **Brian:** Let me start with a question for all of you. How many subscriptions do YOU pay for right now? Think about it – Netflix? Spotify? ChatGPT? Cloud storage? Gaming? Adobe?
>
> Now here's the real question: Do you actually KNOW how much you're spending each month?
>
> According to Cobzaru and Tugui, who published research on the subscription economy in 2024, consumers now spend an average of one hundred thirty-three US dollars per month on subscriptions. That's over sixteen hundred dollars every year – just on subscriptions!

---

# SLIDE 4: Background – The Big Picture

> 🟢 **Brian:** And this isn't a small trend. According to the same researchers, the subscription economy is growing at eighteen percent annually. By 2025, it will reach a market value of one point five trillion dollars. Think about it – everything is becoming a subscription now.
>
> So why does this matter for students? According to Kennedy and colleagues from their 2008 study on technology use among university students, nearly all university students own smartphones – that's ninety-six percent – and computers – that's eighty-nine percent. Students are early adopters of technology.
>
> But here's the problem – most of us have limited budgets. So how do students navigate this subscription-heavy world? That's exactly what we wanted to find out.

---

# SLIDE 5: Research Objectives

> 🟠 **Rex:** Our research had four main goals. First, we wanted to identify what subscription services HKU SPACE students actually use. Second, we wanted to understand what factors influence their decisions to subscribe or cancel. Third, we wanted to evaluate their awareness of cost-saving strategies. And fourth, we wanted to assess their overall attitude toward the subscription model.
>
> So the big question we're asking is: Do students love subscriptions, tolerate them, or secretly hate them? Let's find out.

---

# SLIDE 6: Method

> 🟠 **Rex:** Now let me explain how we collected our data. We used a Microsoft Forms questionnaire. Our target was full-time HKU SPACE students. We distributed the survey between March ninth and March twentieth, 2026. We received thirty-seven responses.
>
> Our questionnaire had twenty-four questions covering six sections: demographics, usage patterns, decision factors, cancellation behaviors, payment preferences, and cost-saving strategies.

---

# SLIDE 7: Demographics

> 🔴 **Ali:** Let me tell you about who responded to our survey.
>
> In terms of programme, most of our respondents – twenty-four students, which is sixty-five percent – were from Full-time Associate Degree programmes. Seven students were from Higher Diploma, five from Diploma, and one from Foundation.
>
> For gender, we had twenty-five male students – that's sixty-eight percent – and eleven female students – thirty percent. One student preferred not to say.
>
> Now, here's a really important number. Look at monthly spending. Twenty-seven students – that's seventy-three percent of our respondents – spend between two thousand and one and four thousand Hong Kong dollars per month. This is our key group.
>
> According to Iyengar and colleagues from their 2022 study on subscription programs, this spending pattern is typical for Hong Kong community college students who receive family support or work part-time. Keep this in mind – it shapes everything else we found.

---

# SLIDE 8: Usage Patterns – The Big Number

> 🔴 **Ali:** So how many students actually use subscriptions? Here's the big number.
>
> Eighty-six percent of our respondents have used an online subscription service. That's thirty-two out of thirty-seven students. Only five students said they've never used one.
>
> According to Yoganarasimhan and colleagues from their 2023 study on free trials, this reflects a broader trend – young adults in Asia have dramatically increased their digital content consumption since the pandemic.
>
> So yes, students ARE using subscriptions. But what exactly are they paying for? Let me show you.

# SLIDE 11: The Ownership Paradox – Introduction

> 🟣 **Anson:** Now here's where things get really interesting. I want to introduce you to something called the "Ownership Paradox."
>
> Students USE subscriptions... but do they actually LIKE the subscription model?
>
> According to Yoganarasimhan and colleagues from their 2023 study, there's a fascinating contradiction in digital consumption. They write, and I quote: "Consumers subscribe to digital products not because they prefer them, but because they are convenient or necessary."
>
> Let me show you what our data revealed.

---

# SLIDE 12: Payment Preferences – Chart

> 🟣 **Anson:** Take a look at this chart. It shows spending allowance compared to payment method preference.

---

# SLIDE 13: Payment Preferences – Analysis

> 🟣 **Anson:** Here's what we found. When asked about payment preferences for software and tools, thirty-one percent strongly prefer one-time purchases. Another forty-one percent somewhat prefer one-time purchases. That means OVER SEVENTY PERCENT prefer one-time purchases.
>
> Compare that to only thirteen percent who somewhat prefer subscriptions.
>
> Let me put this simply. Students use subscriptions because they're convenient or necessary. But given a choice? They'd rather buy something once and own it forever.

---

# SLIDE 14: The HKD 2,001-4,000 Group – Key Finding

> 🟣 **Anson:** Remember the twenty-seven students spending between two thousand and one and four thousand Hong Kong dollars monthly? This is our largest group – seventy-three percent of respondents.
>
> Look at their payment preferences. Seventeen of them preferred one-time purchases. Only four preferred subscriptions.
>
> According to Neunhoeffer from a 2023 study on subscription traps, this makes sense – consumers struggle to predict the long-term value of subscriptions. When your budget is tight, paying once gives you certainty. Subscriptions feel risky.

---

# SLIDE 15: Why Students Cancel – Chart

> 🟡 **Lihe:** Now let's look at why students hit that cancel button.

---

# SLIDE 16: Cancellation Analysis

> 🟡 **Lihe:** The number one reason for cancellation? Not using the service. Thirty-eight percent of students – twelve people – said "infrequency of use" was their primary reason. Thirty-one percent said they found a better alternative. Twenty-five percent said price increase or too expensive.
>
> What does this tell us? According to Datta and colleagues from their 2015 study on customer retention with free trials, subscriptions need to continuously demonstrate value to keep customers – especially cost-conscious students.
>
> If you're not using it, you cancel. Simple as that.

---

# SLIDE 17: Low-Cost Subscriptions – Chart

> 🟡 **Lihe:** Now here's something surprising. Take a look at this chart about low-cost subscriptions.

---

# SLIDE 18: Low-Cost Analysis

> 🟡 **Lihe:** We asked students: would you keep a subscription under thirty Hong Kong dollars PER YEAR? That's less than a cup of coffee. Super cheap, right?
>
> But look at the responses. Nine students said they were "very likely" to keep it. Nine students said they were "very unlikely" to keep it. Even split.
>
> What's happening here? According to Nguyen from a 2025 study on subscription fatigue, even low-cost subscriptions create mental burden – you have to track them, remember them, manage them.
>
> Students aren't just paying with money. They're paying with mental energy.

---

# SLIDE 19: The Forgotten Renewal Problem – Chart

# SLIDE 20: Forgotten Renewal Analysis

> 🔵 **Tim:** How do students track their subscriptions? Thirteen students use manual tracking like spreadsheets or notes. Twelve students rely only on bank statements. And here's the scary part – twenty-two of these twenty-five students were charged for a forgotten subscription.
>
> Now here's the really interesting part. The ONE student who used a dedicated budgeting app? Zero unexpected charges.
>
> According to Nguyen's 2025 research on subscription fatigue, manual tracking is prone to human error and mental exhaustion. This explains why students are so cautious about taking on more subscriptions.

---

# SLIDE 21: Cost-Saving Strategies – Chart

> 🟢 **Brian:** Now let's look at how students fight back. This chart shows awareness versus active use of cost-saving strategies.

---

# SLIDE 22: Cost-Saving Analysis

> 🟢 **Brian:** The most used strategy is student discounts. Twenty-seven students were aware of this strategy. Nineteen actively used it. And sixteen of those nineteen are in the two thousand and one to four thousand Hong Kong dollar spending group.
>
> The second most used strategy is account sharing. Eleven students share with friends. Eleven students share family plans.
>
> According to Lee and colleagues from their 2024 study on password-sharing behavior, students rationalize account sharing as a form of "community solidarity" – they're helping each other out.
>
> Other strategies: nine students set reminders to cancel free trials. But only four use bundles.

# SLIDE 23: Advantages vs Disadvantages – Chart

---

# SLIDE 24: Advantages vs Disadvantages – Analysis

> 🟠 **Rex:** For advantages, convenience and continuous access was the top choice – sixteen students. Access to a wide range of content came second – fourteen students.
>
> For disadvantages, the top concern was clear – accumulation of ongoing costs. Eighteen students selected this as the primary disadvantage. Other concerns included lack of permanent ownership and paying for unused services.
>
> According to Hsiao and Chen from their 2017 study on e-book subscription services, only four students thought subscriptions are more cost-effective than one-time purchases.
>
> Students see the convenience... but they KNOW the costs add up.

# SLIDE 26: Overall Attitudes – Analysis

> 🔴 **Ali:** Here's what we found. Sixteen students said their attitude is "slightly negative." Six said "very negative." That's twenty-two negative responses. Only seven said "slightly positive." Only one said "very positive."
>
> Twenty-two negative versus eight positive.
>
> According to Baek and Kim from their 2022 study on consumer perceptions of subscription models, when products change to subscription models, purchase intentions shift from "yes" to "no" – users rely on actual experience rather than pre-purchase information.
>
> Students use subscriptions. But they don't have to LIKE the trend. Frequent use does NOT mean strong support.

---

# SLIDE 27: Recommendations – Systematic

> 🟣 **Anson:** So what can be done? Let me start with recommendations at the systematic level.
>
> Based on the European Commission's 2024 research on digital fairness, we have three main recommendations.
>
> First, require transparent billing and pricing – no hidden fees, clear renewal dates.
>
> Second, ban deceptive website designs – no manipulative "dark patterns," no tricky cancellation flows.
>
> Third, require simple cancellation – one-click cancellation, same ease as signing up.
>
> The proposed EU Digital Fairness Act addresses exactly these issues. Hong Kong could benefit from similar regulations.

---

# SLIDE 28: Recommendations – Personal

> 🟡 **Lihe:** Now let me talk about what students can do themselves, based on Nguyen's 2025 research on subscription fatigue.
>
> First, use subscription tracking tools. Remember – the one student who used a budgeting app had ZERO forgotten renewals.
>
> Second, check the final price before checkout – watch for sneaky fees or added items.
>
> Third, set reminders for free trials – nine students already do this. Learn from them!
>
> And fourth, improve your financial and digital literacy through research and self-training.
>
> Better business practices plus smarter personal habits equals less stressful subscriptions.

---

# SLIDE 29: Limitations

> 🔵 **Tim:** Before we conclude, let's be honest about our study's limitations.
>
> First, we have a small sample size – only thirty-seven students. This may not represent all HKU SPACE students.
>
> Second, this is self-reported data. People don't always remember accurately, and there's something called social desirability bias – the tendency to give answers that make you look good rather than being fully honest.
>
> Third, this is a single institution study. Our results may not apply to other universities in Hong Kong.
>
> Fourth, this is a snapshot in time. We can't track how attitudes change over a student's academic career or in response to economic shifts.

---

# SLIDE 30: Conclusion

> 🔵 **Tim:** So what did we learn? Let me summarize our key findings.
>
> First, HKU SPACE students are active subscription users – especially for entertainment.
>
> Second, they approach subscriptions with caution and pragmatism.
>
> Third, cost is the main reason for cancellation – but even cheap subscriptions get cancelled if not used.
>
> Fourth, they prefer one-time purchases to avoid the stress of tracking recurring payments.
>
> Fifth, they use smart strategies – discounts, sharing, trial reminders.
>
> And finally, the overall attitude toward the subscription trend is generally negative. Twenty-two negative versus eight positive. Frequent use does NOT mean strong support.

---

# SLIDE 31: Final Takeaway

> 🔵 **Tim:** My final takeaway is this: Students are strategic consumers.
>
> They use subscriptions when convenient or necessary. But given a choice? They'd rather buy once and own it forever.
>
> For service providers, this means you need to respect students' budget-conscious approach, demonstrate value continuously, and make cancellation easy – because that actually builds trust.
>
> For students, use tracking tools, set reminders, and remember – you don't have to love subscriptions just because you use them.
