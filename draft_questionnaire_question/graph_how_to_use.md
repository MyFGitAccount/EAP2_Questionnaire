### 2.2 Usage Patterns and Service Preferences

**Recommended Images:**
- `1_programme_vs_usage.png` — Shows subscription adoption rates by programme
- `2_programme_vs_service_types.png` — Shows service preferences across programmes
- `3_spending_vs_usage.png` — Shows subscription adoption by spending allowance
- `4_spending_vs_service_types.png` — Shows service preferences by spending level

**How to Write:**

> **Subscription Adoption**
>
> Overall subscription adoption was high, with 86.5% of respondents (n=32) reporting prior or current use of online subscription services (Figure 1). Adoption rates varied by programme category: Diploma and Foundation students demonstrated universal adoption (100%), while Associate Degree students showed strong adoption at 91.7%. Notably, Higher Diploma students exhibited substantially lower adoption at 57.1% (Figure 1). This disparity may reflect differences in digital engagement, academic requirements, or financial priorities across programmes.
>
> When analyzed by spending allowance (Figure 3), subscription adoption was near-universal among respondents with allowances below HKD $6,000, with 100% adoption in the lowest two brackets. However, adoption dropped to 50% among respondents with allowances exceeding HKD $6,000. This counterintuitive finding may indicate that higher-income students either prefer one-time purchases or that the small sample size (n=4) in this bracket limits generalizability.
>
> **Service Type Preferences**
>
> Entertainment services dominated the subscription landscape. Video streaming (19 mentions, 59% of users) and music streaming (18 mentions, 56%) were the most frequently used categories, followed by software/cloud storage (14 mentions, 44%), gaming (12 mentions, 38%), and e-learning (11 mentions, 34%). This pattern reflects the dual role of subscriptions in student life: primarily for leisure and entertainment, with secondary utility for academic and productivity needs.
>
> Programme-based analysis revealed distinct service preferences (Figure 2). Associate Degree students showed the most diverse engagement, with video streaming (14), music streaming (13), and gaming (10) as top choices—consistent with entertainment-focused consumption. Diploma students, in contrast, prioritized software/cloud storage (3) and e-learning (2), suggesting greater emphasis on academic and productivity tools. Higher Diploma students demonstrated a strong preference for music streaming (4), with moderate engagement across other categories. These differences may reflect varying academic demands, digital literacy levels, or personal interests across programmes.
>
> When analyzed by spending allowance (Figure 4), the HKD $2,001–$4,000 group demonstrated the widest and most active engagement across all service categories. This suggests that students with moderate allowances are the most active subscription consumers, potentially because they have sufficient disposable income to participate while remaining price-sensitive enough to seek value.

### 2.3 Factors Influencing Subscription and Cancellation Decisions

**Recommended Images:**
- `6_programme_vs_cancel_reason.png` — Shows cancellation reasons by programme
- `27_cancel_reason_vs_advantage.png` — Shows relationship between cancellation reasons and perceived advantages
- `28_cancel_reason_vs_overall_view.png` — Shows how cancellation reasons relate to overall attitudes

**How to Write:**

> **Cancellation Drivers**
>
> The primary reason cited for canceling a paid subscription was infrequency of use, selected by 12 respondents (32.4%). This was followed by finding a better alternative (10 respondents, 27.0%) and price increases (8 respondents, 21.6%). Notably, only one respondent cited financial pressure as the primary reason, and none cited content quality decline (Figure 6).
>
> This pattern indicates that students are not inherently opposed to subscriptions on principle but rather evaluate them based on ongoing value delivery. When a subscription fails to be used regularly or when a superior alternative emerges, cancellation follows. Price sensitivity, while present, appears secondary to perceived value-for-usage. This finding challenges the assumption that students are purely cost-driven and suggests that service providers can retain student subscribers by maintaining high engagement and usage frequency.
>
> Programme-based variations were observed (Figure 6). Associate Degree students most frequently cited infrequency of use (8) and better alternatives (7), while Diploma students showed more diverse reasons including price increases and technical problems. These differences may reflect varying service portfolios across programmes.
>
> **Relationship Between Cancellation and Perceptions**
>
> Cross-analysis of cancellation reasons against perceived advantages (Figure 27) revealed alignment between behavior and values. Respondents who cited infrequency of use as their primary cancellation reason disproportionately valued convenience and continuous access (6) and broad content access (3). This suggests that when the convenience or content variety no longer justifies regular use, the perceived value collapses, prompting cancellation.
>
> Similarly, those who canceled due to finding better alternatives valued broad content access (5) and convenience (4), indicating that competition in these dimensions drives churn. Interestingly, those who canceled due to price increases were equally split between valuing convenience and broad content access, suggesting that price sensitivity is independent of what users value most.
>
> Regarding overall attitudes toward the subscription trend (Figure 28), respondents who canceled due to infrequency of use or finding better alternatives were overrepresented in the "somewhat negative" category, while those canceling due to price increases showed a more even distribution across sentiment categories. This suggests that negative attitudes are shaped not only by cost concerns but also by experiences of underutilization and perceived inefficiency.

### 2.4 Cost-Saving Strategies and Payment Preferences

**How to Write:**

> **Payment Method Preferences**
>
> A strong preference for one-time purchases emerged across the sample. Combined, 62% of respondents (n=23) indicated they somewhat or strongly prefer one-time purchases over subscriptions for software and tools, with no respondents indicating a strong preference for subscriptions (Figure 11). This clear preference for ownership over recurring payments reflects a fundamental skepticism toward the subscription model among student consumers.
>
> When analyzed by spending allowance (Figure 11), preferences remained consistent across income brackets. Even among the moderate-allowance group (HKD $2,001–$4,000), one-time purchase preferences dominated (17 respondents versus 4 preferring subscriptions). This consistency suggests that preference for ownership is not solely driven by financial constraints but reflects a broader consumer value orientation.
>
> Cross-analysis of low-cost subscription behavior against payment preference (Figure 12) revealed an interesting pattern. Respondents who indicated they were "very likely" to keep a low-cost subscription even if rarely used showed a nearly even split between one-time purchase preferences and subscription preferences. This suggests that users who are comfortable with low-cost "set-and-forget" subscriptions may be more open to subscription models generally, whereas those who are "very unlikely" to keep unused low-cost subscriptions strongly prefer one-time purchases (8 respondents versus 0 preferring subscriptions). This indicates that conscientiousness about subscription waste is strongly correlated with ownership preferences.

### 2.5 Cost-Saving Strategies

**Recommended Images:**
- `18_awareness_vs_active_strategies.png` — Compares awareness versus active use of cost-saving strategies
- `19_spending_vs_active_strategies.png` — Shows active strategy usage by spending allowance

**How to Write:**

> **Awareness Versus Action**
>
> Cost-saving strategies showed varying levels of awareness and adoption among respondents (Figure 18). Student discounts had the highest awareness (27 respondents) and also the highest active usage (19 respondents), indicating that this is both well-known and effectively utilized. Family plans and account sharing similarly showed strong conversion from awareness to action, suggesting that students actively leverage their social networks and household structures to reduce subscription costs.
>
> However, a significant awareness-action gap exists for certain strategies. Bundles, despite being known to 7 respondents, were actively used by only 4. More notably, rotating services (subscribing for one month and switching) had awareness among only 2 respondents and active usage by none, representing an underutilized strategy that could yield substantial savings. Pausing subscriptions showed a conversion rate of 50% (3 of 6 aware users actively using), indicating that those who know about this option tend to use it effectively.
>
> **Strategy Usage by Spending Allowance**
>
> Active strategy usage varied meaningfully by spending allowance (Figure 19). The moderate-allowance group (HKD $2,001–$4,000) was the most active across all strategy categories, with 16 using student discounts, 10 using account sharing, and 9 using family plans. This group also showed the most diverse strategy portfolio, including setting reminders to cancel free trials (8 users).
>
> In contrast, the lowest-allowance group (less than HKD $2,000) showed limited strategy engagement, with only 2 using student discounts and scattered usage of other strategies. This may indicate that the most financially constrained students either lack awareness of strategies, face barriers to accessing them, or have such limited disposable income that they avoid subscriptions altogether rather than seeking to optimize them. The highest-allowance group (more than HKD $6,000) showed minimal engagement with cost-saving strategies, consistent with their lower overall subscription adoption and potentially reduced need to economize.

### 2.6 Perceptions of the Subscription Model

**Recommended Images:**
- `8_spending_vs_overall_view.png` — Shows overall attitudes by spending allowance
- `9_spending_vs_disadvantage.png` — Shows perceived disadvantages by spending allowance
- `13_savings_vs_overall_view.png` — Shows relationship between cost-savings perception and overall attitudes
- `22_service_types_vs_advantage.png` — Shows perceived advantages by service type

**How to Write:**

> **Overall Attitudes**
>
> The overall sentiment toward the subscription model trend was predominantly negative. Combined, 59.5% of respondents (n=22) expressed somewhat negative (43.2%) or very negative (16.2%) views, while only 21.6% expressed somewhat or very positive views (Figure 8). This distribution indicates significant consumer resistance to the broader shift from ownership to access models.
>
> When analyzed by spending allowance (Figure 8), negative sentiment was most concentrated in the moderate-allowance group (HKD $2,001–$4,000), where 14 respondents expressed somewhat negative views and 2 expressed very negative views. The lowest-allowance group showed the highest proportion of very negative sentiment (2 out of 4), suggesting that financial constraints intensify negative attitudes. Interestingly, the highest-allowance group also showed elevated very negative sentiment (2 out of 4), indicating that negative perceptions are not exclusively driven by financial limitations.
>
> **Perceived Advantages and Disadvantages**
>
> The perceived primary advantage of subscriptions was split between convenience and continuous access (16 respondents, 43.2%) and access to a broad range of content or features (14 respondents, 37.8%) (Figure 22). Service-specific analysis revealed that video and music streaming users were more likely to cite broad content access, while software and e-learning users emphasized convenience—reflecting the distinct value propositions of entertainment versus productivity subscriptions.
>
> The dominant perceived disadvantage was the accumulation of recurring costs (18 respondents, 48.6%), followed by lack of permanent ownership (8 respondents, 21.6%) and potential for paying for unused services (6 respondents, 16.2%) (Figure 9). When analyzed by spending allowance (Figure 9), the accumulation of recurring costs was most concerning for the moderate-allowance group (13 respondents), while lack of permanent ownership was more prominent among lower and higher allowance groups. This suggests that financial concerns dominate for middle-income students, while philosophical concerns about ownership resonate across income extremes.
>
> **Relationship Between Cost Perception and Attitudes**
>
> A strong correlation emerged between belief in the cost-effectiveness of subscriptions and overall attitudes (Figure 13). Among respondents who agreed that subscriptions save money in the long run, 4 held neutral views and 3 held somewhat negative views, with none holding very negative views. Conversely, among those who disagreed or strongly disagreed that subscriptions save money, negative views predominated: 10 somewhat negative and 4 very negative across the two groups. This indicates that fundamental beliefs about the financial value proposition of subscriptions are a key determinant of overall sentiment toward the model.

### 2.7 User Experience

**Recommended Images:**
- `15_change_plan_vs_disadvantage.png` — Shows relationship between ease of plan changes and perceived disadvantages
- `16_convenience_vs_monitoring.png` — Shows relationship between convenience ratings and payment monitoring methods
- `25_forgotten_renewal_vs_monitoring.png` — Shows relationship between forgotten renewals and monitoring methods
- `26_forgotten_renewal_vs_overall_view.png` — Shows relationship between forgotten renewals and overall attitudes

**How to Write:**

> **Friction Points in Subscription Management**
>
> User experience friction was evident across multiple dimensions of subscription management. A concerning 70.3% of respondents (n=26) reported having been charged for a subscription renewal because they did not cancel before the free trial or renewal date (Figure 25). This high rate suggests either widespread difficulty in navigating cancellation processes or insufficient awareness of renewal dates—or both.
>
> Payment monitoring habits were strongly correlated with forgotten renewals (Figure 25). Among respondents who had experienced unexpected charges, 11 relied on manual tracking (spreadsheets or notes) and 4 did not actively monitor their subscriptions. In contrast, among those who had not experienced unexpected charges, only 2 used manual tracking and 2 did not monitor. Notably, all respondents who used budgeting or subscription-tracking applications (n=1) and the majority of those relying on bank statements or email notifications (11 of 12) had experienced forgotten renewals, indicating that even relatively active monitoring does not fully prevent the issue.
>
> **Cancellation and Plan Management**
>
> Difficulty in finding cancellation options was reported by 17 respondents (46%), with 4 describing it as "very difficult" (Figure 15). This friction point translates directly into the high rate of unexpected charges observed. Similarly, ease of making plan changes varied widely, with 11 finding it easy, 8 neutral, and 8 finding it difficult or very difficult (Figure 15).
>
> Analysis of the relationship between plan change difficulty and perceived disadvantages (Figure 15) revealed that respondents who found plan changes difficult were more likely to cite "difficulty or complexity in cancelling" as their primary disadvantage (1 out of 6 in the difficult group versus 0 in the easy group). However, the majority of those who found plan changes difficult still cited accumulation of recurring costs as their primary concern, suggesting that cancellation friction is understood as a contributor to the broader cost accumulation problem.
>
> **Convenience Versus Active Management**
>
> The convenience of automatic payments received mixed ratings (Figure 16). While 12 respondents rated it "about the same" as manual buying and 7 rated it "slightly more convenient" or "much more convenient," 13 rated it "slightly less convenient" or "much less convenient." This split reflects the dual nature of subscription convenience: automatic payments reduce transactional friction but simultaneously reduce oversight and control.
>
> Payment monitoring methods varied significantly by convenience perception (Figure 16). Respondents who found subscriptions "much less convenient" were more likely to rely on manual tracking or not monitor at all, suggesting that those who prefer hands-on financial management may experience subscriptions as an imposition rather than a convenience.
>
> **Impact on Overall Attitudes**
>
> The experience of forgotten renewals had a substantial impact on overall attitudes toward the subscription model (Figure 26). Among respondents who had experienced unexpected charges, 15 held somewhat negative views and 4 held very negative views—a combined 73% negative sentiment. In contrast, among those who had not experienced unexpected charges, 4 held somewhat negative views and none held very negative views. This indicates that negative user experiences, particularly around unexpected charges, are powerful drivers of negative sentiment toward the subscription model overall.

### 2.8 Overall Attitudes

**Recommended Images:**
- `8_spending_vs_overall_view.png` — Overall attitudes by spending allowance
- `13_savings_vs_overall_view.png` — Relationship between cost-savings perception and attitudes
- `26_forgotten_renewal_vs_overall_view.png` — Relationship between forgotten renewals and attitudes
- `28_cancel_reason_vs_overall_view.png` — Relationship between cancellation reasons and attitudes
- `29_flexibility_vs_convenience_matrix.png` — Perceptual segmentation of users

**How to Write:**

> **Summary of Attitudes**
>
> The overall attitude toward the shift to subscription-based models was predominantly negative, with 59.5% of respondents expressing somewhat or very negative views. Only 21.6% expressed somewhat or very positive views, while 18.9% remained neutral (Figure 8). This distribution reflects a student population that engages with subscriptions out of necessity or convenience but views the broader trend with skepticism.
>
> **Determinants of Attitudes**
>
> Three key factors emerged as significant determinants of overall attitudes:
>
> First, belief in the cost-effectiveness of subscriptions strongly predicted attitudes (Figure 13). Among respondents who disagreed or strongly disagreed that subscriptions save money, negative views predominated (10 somewhat negative, 4 very negative). Among those who agreed, attitudes were more balanced, with 4 neutral, 3 somewhat negative, and 1 somewhat positive. This suggests that skepticism about the financial value proposition is a core driver of negative sentiment.
>
> Second, negative user experiences—particularly forgotten renewals—substantially shaped attitudes (Figure 26). The 73% negative sentiment among those who had experienced unexpected charges contrasts sharply with the 0% very negative sentiment among those who had not. This finding highlights the importance of transparent cancellation processes and clear renewal notifications in shaping consumer perceptions.
>
> Third, cancellation reasons correlated with attitudes (Figure 28). Those who canceled due to infrequency of use or finding better alternatives showed higher concentrations of negative sentiment compared to those who canceled due to price increases. This suggests that dissatisfaction with usage patterns or perceived value, rather than price alone, may drive more entrenched negative attitudes.
>
> **Perceptual Segmentation**
>
> The perceptual gap analysis between flexibility and convenience (Figure 29) revealed distinct user segments. "Power users"—who found subscriptions both more flexible and more convenient—represented a small minority (approximately 4 respondents). "Skeptics"—who found subscriptions less flexible and less convenient—represented a larger segment (approximately 5 respondents across various skeptic categories). "Reluctant users"—who found subscriptions less flexible but more convenient—also represented a notable segment (approximately 2 respondents).
>
> This segmentation suggests that while a minority of students fully embrace the subscription model for its flexibility and convenience, a substantial proportion views it with ambivalence or skepticism. The prevalence of skeptic segments aligns with the overall negative sentiment distribution and reinforces that the subscription model is not universally perceived as an improvement over traditional ownership.
>
> **Conclusion**
>
> The findings collectively indicate that HKUSPACE students are active subscription users but reluctant adopters of the subscription model. They engage with subscriptions primarily for entertainment and, to a lesser extent, academic purposes, but they do so while maintaining a strong preference for one-time purchases. Negative experiences—particularly unexpected charges and difficulty canceling—substantially shape attitudes, as does skepticism about the long-term cost-effectiveness of subscriptions.
>
> For service providers and institutions, these findings suggest that improving transparency around cancellation processes, providing clearer renewal notifications, and offering flexible payment options could improve user satisfaction and potentially shift attitudes. For students, greater awareness of cost-saving strategies—particularly rotating services and pausing rather than canceling—could reduce financial waste and improve perceived value.
